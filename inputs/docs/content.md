# ETEC511 65A Knowledge Base

## 01. Overview of the Tool

The Question Coach is a pedagogical system designed to support learners in developing clear, focused, and purposeful questions through the Question Formulation Technique (QFT). The system comprises three interconnected components:

- **AI Question Coach:** A language model-based conversational agent that guides learners through the QFT process using Socratic questioning. The coach does not generate questions or answers, but prompts reflection and refinement.
- **Physical Prompt Cards:** Analog intervention cards drawn at specific stages of the QFT process. These cards introduce intentional interruptions to the digital workflow, encouraging reflection and divergent thinking.
- **Knowledge Base:** This handbook provides comprehensive guidance on the system's purpose, theoretical foundation, and practical application.

The tool introduces *intentional cognitive friction*—strategic obstacles that slow thinking and resist outsourcing cognition to AI systems. Unlike tools optimized for speed, the Question Coach is optimized for depth, reflection, and learner agency.

## 02. The Problem: The Disappearance of the Unclear Question

UNESCO's 2023 guidance on generative AI in education identifies a critical concern: students increasingly bypass question formulation. Instead of refining vague ideas into clear questions, learners submit poorly formed queries to AI and accept the output.

This is a pedagogical loss. Moving from unclear to clear question is not preparatory work—it is thinking itself. Outsourcing this process reduces development of critical thinking, metacognition, and intellectual independence.

The Question Coach addresses this by making question formulation visible, structured, and unavoidable.

## 03. Ethical Position: AI as Scaffold, Not Substitute

- The AI does not generate questions for the learner; intellectual work remains with the student.
- The system refuses shortcuts (no ready-made thesis/research questions).
- Friction is a feature: slowing down and reflecting is intentional.
- Learner agency is preserved in prioritization and refinement decisions.

This positions AI as a thinking partner, not a replacement for learner cognition.

## 04. System Architecture

The Question Coach integrates digital and analog components with intentionally simple architecture focused on cognition over technical complexity.

**Flow:** Student -> AI Coach -> Prompt Cards -> Reflection

The student begins with a question focus. The coach guides QFT stages. Prompt cards are drawn at transitions. The process ends in prioritization and metacognitive reflection.

## 05. What is the Question Formulation Technique (QFT)

QFT is a structured process (Right Question Institute) to formulate, refine, and prioritize learner-generated questions.

1. **Question Focus:** Prompt or topic stimulus.
2. **Generate Questions:** Ask many questions; do not answer/discuss; record exactly; convert statements to questions.
3. **Categorize Questions:** Distinguish closed and open questions.
4. **Change Question Types:** Convert open/closed forms.
5. **Prioritize Questions:** Select important questions based on criteria.
6. **Reflect:** Consider what was learned and next actions.

## 06. How to Use the Question Coach

1. **Begin with a Question Focus:** Identify a topic or prompt and share with coach.
2. **Generate Questions:** Follow QFT rules. *Draw one Stage 2 prompt card before moving on.*
3. **Categorize Questions:** Identify open vs closed.
4. **Change Question Types:** Convert selected questions. *Draw one Stage 4 prompt card before moving on.*
5. **Prioritize Questions:** Select top three and justify criteria.
6. **Reflect:** Explain learning, changes in understanding, and next use of questions.

## 07. AI Question Coach: Behaviour and Constraints

- Uses Socratic questioning to provoke reasoning and reflection.
- Does not generate learner questions or provide final answers.
- Encourages reflection at stage transitions.
- Refuses shortcuts and redirects to learner-owned inquiry.
- Handles frustration with transparent explanation of intentional friction.
- Maintains stage integrity to protect divergent thinking.

## 08. Prompt Card System

Physical cards interrupt digital flow to create reflective space.

### Stage 2 examples

- Choose one question. Ask three questions about that question.
- If someone with very different values approached this topic, what would they ask?
- What question about this topic would be taboo or uncomfortable?

### Stage 4 examples

- Which question scares you a little, and why is that significant?
- If you could answer one question this month, which creates most clarity?
- Which closed question is most worth converting to open form?

Analog cards break scrolling/typing momentum, reorient attention, and introduce productive unpredictability.

## 09. Educational Usability vs. Conventional Usability

| Conventional Usability | Educational Usability |
|---|---|
| Minimize user effort | Require effortful thinking |
| Optimize for speed | Optimize for depth |
| Remove obstacles | Introduce productive obstacles |
| Provide immediate answers | Delay resolution for reflection |
| Streamline all interactions | Create pauses and transitions |
| Maximize efficiency | Maximize learning |

Educational usability intentionally uses cognitive load, friction, and desirable difficulties to support deeper learning.

## 10. Configuring the User (Woolgar)

The system configures a learner who thinks slowly, tolerates ambiguity, resists outsourcing cognition, and values inquiry process over answer efficiency.

- Encourages slow reflective thinking.
- Prevents outsourcing thought to AI.
- Structures action through constraints.
- Normalizes productive struggle as part of learning.

## 11. What Counts as Successful Use

- Completion of all six QFT stages.
- Improved clarity and specificity of prioritized questions.
- Visible metacognitive reflection.
- Reduced attempts to use AI for direct answers.
- Productive engagement with friction.

## 12. Anticipated Failure Modes

- Bypassing prompt cards: require specific evidence of engagement.
- Trying to force AI to generate questions: maintain refusal boundaries and prompt reflection on resistance.
- Seeing friction as poor UX: explicitly frame friction as educational design.
- Using external AI shortcuts: probe learner reasoning to reveal ownership gaps.
- Mechanical completion without reflection: use deeper follow-up prompts and educator review.

## 13. Usability Assessment Plan

- Think-aloud walkthroughs: observe hesitation, confusion, and bypass attempts.
- Behavioral observation: track completion, card use, and struggle patterns.
- Post-use interviews: collect friction/value perceptions and learning insights.
- Question quality analysis: compare early vs final questions for clarity/depth.

**Success indicators:** friction tolerance, better questions, articulated process value, and fewer shortcut attempts over time.
